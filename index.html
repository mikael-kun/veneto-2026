Wednesday · 8 July

Arrival, and the first spritz

Top 9 Jul →

The alarm went off at 5:00 a.m., which always feels a little cruel no matter how exciting the destination. A Bolt picked us up at 05:35, and before long we found ourselves sleepily making our way through Helsinki Airport. It quickly became obvious we weren't the only ones heading south—large Japanese tour groups filled the departure hall, and somehow it felt fitting. This trip was never going to be about escaping the crowds, but about discovering the stories hidden among them.

AY1781 departed right on schedule, and a few hours later we landed at Venice Marco Polo Airport. After the on-shore ritual of finding the ATVO ticket counter and buying return bus tickets before the queues grew any longer, we made our way to lane D2. From there the coach carried us across the lagoon to Piazzale Roma, where Venice truly begins. Cars disappear, canals take over, and every journey continues on foot—or by boat.

Our first stop in Venice was La Lista, just off Campo San Geremia. A couple of bruschette and cappuccinos were exactly what we needed after the early start. It was nice to simply sit for a while and watch the city wake up around us before setting off to explore on foot. Venice has a way of rewarding those moments when you slow down and let the atmosphere sink in.

After breakfast we made our way to our apartment, P 1779. One of the nicest surprises was that our b'n'b host, Max, gave us an excellent introduction to Venice. Instead of the usual tourist brochure, it was advice from someone who genuinely knows the city—where to eat, which wine bars were worth visiting, where to find the best viewpoints, and where Venetians actually shop. Those tips ended up shaping much of our stay over the following days.

From there we simply started walking. Like so many first days in Venice, we didn't really have a destination in mind. Instead, we let the narrow calli lead the way, turning corners whenever something looked interesting. Before long we found ourselves near San Sofia, where we stopped for our first spritz of the trip. Sitting by the canal, watching tourists drift past as the afternoon light danced across the water, it felt like we'd finally arrived.

After a quick stop back at P 1779 to freshen up, we headed out again, this time through the Ghetto Nuovo. Compared to the busy streets around Rialto and San Marco, Cannaregio felt noticeably calmer. The buildings seemed taller, the crowds thinner, and the pace slower. It was one of those places where Venice reveals a different side of itself—less about grand landmarks and more about everyday life quietly unfolding between the canals.

For our first dinner in Venice we had booked Osteria Anice Stellato, and it turned out to be an excellent introduction to the city's food and wine. While every dish was beautifully prepared, what really stood out was how thoughtfully the wines had been paired with each course. It felt like the wines were just as much a part of the experience as the food itself.

We began with a lightly sparkling Lambrusco alongside smoked tomato and anchovy, followed by vibrant green snail gnocchi paired with a red from Sassi di Matera. The final course was spaghettone with mussels, accompanied by a crisp glass of Verdicchio. Looking back, it was the perfect first evening: relaxed, unhurried, and a reminder that in Italy, a great meal is often as much about what's in the glass as what's on the plate.

Thursday · 9 July

Doges, Etruscans, and a French rout

← 8 Jul Top 10 Jul →

It was one of those mornings where the early start from Wednesday finally caught up with us. A leisurely breakfast in the courtyard was exactly what we needed to reset before another day of exploring. Eggs, cured meats, local cheeses, fresh fruit, and a couple of cappuccinos each provided plenty of energy, even if we had to politely compete with a few persistent wasps that seemed just as interested in the jam as we were.

After breakfast we made our way through Cannaregio towards San Marco, keeping one eye on the time. Our morning revolved around two of Venice's greatest landmarks, so we wanted to make the most of every minute. We began at the Doge's Palace, its distinctive pink-and-white façade looking even more impressive in the bright morning sun. After exploring the magnificent halls, grand staircases, and lavishly decorated chambers, we crossed the famous Bridge of Sighs into the old prison before continuing to our timed entry at the Museo Correr.

I may have taken far more photographs of painted ceilings than any reasonable person should, but I have absolutely no regrets. Between the extraordinary works of Tintoretto, Veronese, and countless anonymous craftsmen, it was impossible not to keep looking up. Some of Venice's greatest masterpieces aren't hanging on the walls—they're painted above your head.

The Museo Correr ended up being one of the biggest surprises of the trip. I had expected an interesting museum, but not one that would take me on a journey from the ancient Etruscans and Veneti all the way to Napoleon's reshaping of Venice. Along the way were beautifully illustrated Renaissance atlases, the original Fra Mauro World Map, an enormous sixteenth-century painting showing St. Mark's Basilica engulfed in flames, and, rather unexpectedly, a cabinet filled with fossil ammonites. It was exactly the sort of museum where wandering and following your curiosity is far more rewarding than rushing from one highlight to the next.

Some exhibits deserved far more time than I could give them during a single visit. The remarkable coin collection, the magnificent ceiling of the Sansovino Library, and especially the Fra Mauro World Map each sent me down their own historical rabbit holes. Rather than trying to squeeze everything into this journal, I've gathered my notes, photographs, and research into separate articles on the Notes page page.

Bocca di Leone (Lion’s Mouth Complaint Box)

One of the more fascinating details inside the Doge's Palace was this Bocca di Leone, or "Lion's Mouth" complaint box. During the time of the Venetian Republic, citizens could submit written reports here concerning suspected crimes such as tax fraud, corruption, or officials withholding public funds. Although they're often described as anonymous denunciation boxes, the system wasn't quite as arbitrary as legend suggests. Serious accusations generally needed supporting evidence before the authorities would begin an investigation, making them an early attempt at balancing public accountability with due process.

One unexpected moment came when I spotted a view into a part of the Doge's Palace that isn't open to visitors. Beyond the elegant Gothic loggia, the Italian flag overlooked the lagoon towards San Giorgio Maggiore, offering a reminder that the palace isn't simply a museum frozen in time. Parts of the building are still used today for official cultural and administrative purposes, continuing a tradition of public service that stretches back more than a thousand years.

Scala d’Oro (Golden Staircase)

The Scala d'Oro, or Golden Staircase, was far more than a way of moving between floors. It formed the ceremonial route to the Doge's private apartments and the chambers where the Venetian government conducted its business. Designed by Jacopo Sansovino and completed by Antonio Scarpagnino in 1559, the staircase is lavishly decorated with gilded stucco by Alessandro Vittoria and frescoes by Giambattista Franco, transforming an ordinary architectural feature into one of the palace's most impressive displays of power and prestige.

It's impressive how deliberately everything had been designed. Renaissance Venice understood the importance of first impressions, and every visiting ambassador, noble, or senior official climbed these stairs before reaching the heart of government. Surrounded by classical mythology, rich decoration, and symbols celebrating Venice's wisdom, prosperity, and naval power, guests were given a clear message long before a single word of diplomacy was exchanged. Architecture itself became part of the Republic's political theatre.

Sala delle Quattro Porte (Room of the Four Doors)

Rebuilt after the great fire of 1574 from designs by Andrea Palladio and Giovanni Antonio Rusconi, the Sala delle Quattro Porte served as the grand antechamber to the Republic's most important council chambers. This was where ambassadors, foreign envoys, and distinguished visitors waited before being admitted to meet the Venetian government. It didn't take long to realise that the room wasn't simply designed to impress—it was designed to send a message.

The ceiling

Looking up, Tintoretto's magnificent ceiling paintings portray Venice under the protection of the classical gods. Like so much of the Doge's Palace, the artwork is carefully chosen to reinforce the Republic's image as a state guided by wisdom, justice, and divine favour. It wasn't just decoration; it was political storytelling told through art.

The four doors

The room takes its name from its four monumental marble doorways, each framed by richly carved stone and crowned with allegorical sculptures. Behind each doorway lay one of the institutions responsible for governing the Republic. Standing here, I couldn't help thinking that the room itself formed a crossroads of Venetian power—every visitor waited here, and whichever door eventually opened determined where they would go, and often why they had come to Venice in the first place.

Sala del Senato (Senate Chamber)

The Sala del Senato was one of the most important rooms in the Doge's Palace. This was where the Venetian Senate met to oversee the Republic's foreign policy, trade, naval affairs, and finances—the decisions that helped transform Venice into one of the Mediterranean's great maritime powers. Made up of experienced patricians, the Senate managed an empire whose influence stretched from northern Italy to the eastern Mediterranean.

Like so many of the palace's state rooms, the chamber was designed to communicate authority as much as it served a practical purpose. Surrounded by monumental paintings by Tintoretto and other Renaissance masters, senators debated beneath scenes celebrating wisdom, justice, and divine guidance. The message was unmistakable: Venice presented itself as a republic whose prosperity and power rested on wealth and military strength, and strived to base it on on wise and stable government.

Sala del Maggior Consiglio (Chamber of the Great Council)

Walking into the Sala del Maggior Consiglio was one of those moments that genuinely made us stop in our tracks. Measuring 53 metres long and 25 metres wide, it's one of the largest historic council chambers in Europe. More than a thousand members of the Venetian nobility once gathered here to elect officials, approve legislation, and debate matters that shaped the future of the Republic.

After the devastating fire of 1577, the hall was rebuilt on an equally ambitious scale and adorned with monumental paintings celebrating Venice's history, victories, and civic ideals. Dominating the far end of the chamber is Tintoretto's Paradise, one of the largest oil paintings ever created. Standing beneath it, it becomes clear that this room was designed not only for government but also to inspire awe. Every painting, every gilded ceiling panel, and every carefully planned detail reinforced the idea that Venice was a republic destined to endure.

Ponte dei Sospiri (The Bridge of Sighs)

The Bridge of Sighs is one of Venice's most photographed landmarks, yet its original purpose was far less romantic than its elegant Baroque appearance suggests. Completed in 1614, it connected the Doge's Palace with the Prigioni Nuove (New Prisons), allowing prisoners to be moved securely between the courtrooms and their cells without ever setting foot outside.

Inside, the bridge is divided into two separate passageways by a central wall. One corridor carried prisoners between the courts and the prison, while the other linked the prison with the offices of the State Advocates. Staircases from the bridge also led to the notorious Pozzi, the damp ground-floor cells, and the Piombi, the prison cells beneath the palace's lead roof.

The name Bridge of Sighs didn't become popular until the Romantic era. According to the legend, condemned prisoners sighed as they caught one last glimpse of Venice through the small stone lattice windows before beginning their sentence. Standing inside the bridge, though, it's easy to see how much of that story belongs to later imagination. The views are surprisingly narrow, offering little more than fleeting glimpses of the canal below rather than the sweeping panorama celebrated by poets and painters. Even so, the narrow glimpse might have felt wider than we can imagine to those facing justice, even like the sweeping panoramas imagined by poets.

San Marco and transition to Museo Correr

Stepping out of the Doge's Palace felt like emerging into a completely different world. After the cool, gilded halls and quiet grandeur of the Republic's council chambers, Piazza San Marco was alive with sunshine, conversations, and the constant flow of people crossing the square. The Campanile cast its long shadow across the paving stones while Sansovino's elegant Loggetta gleamed brilliantly at its base. Even after spending hours inside one of Venice's greatest monuments, the square itself still managed to steal the show.

Our next stop was the Museo Correr, located at the opposite end of the piazza inside the Napoleonic Wing (Ala Napoleonica). Crossing St. Mark's Square isn't really a walk—it's an experience in itself. Every direction offers another detail worth stopping for, whether it's the arcades, the Basilica, or simply watching the rhythm of Venice unfold around you. Before long we stepped inside the museum, unaware it would become one of the highlights of the entire trip.

One of my favourite moments came almost immediately after entering the Museo Correr. After weaving through the crowds in Piazza San Marco and climbing the museum's staircase, the first thing waiting on the upper floor wasn't an exhibition at all—it was the square itself. From the windows, the bustling piazza suddenly felt calm and perfectly framed, with the domes of St. Mark's Basilica and the Campanile rising behind the sea of people below. It was a completely different perspective on a place we'd crossed only moments earlier.

Museo Correr

Correr is not just an art museum, it tells the story of Venice from antiquity through the Republic, Napoleon, and the Habsburg period.

The Origins of the Museum

Museo Correr is much more than an art museum. Rather than focusing on a single period, it tells the story of Venice itself — from the earliest settlements of the Veneti and Etruscans, through the rise of the Republic, and into the Napoleonic and Habsburg eras. Walking through its galleries feels less like visiting a traditional museum and more like following the city's biography across two thousand years.

The museum exists thanks to the vision of Teodoro Correr (1750–1830), one of the last great Venetian patricians. Having witnessed the fall of the Republic in 1797, he saw churches being closed, noble collections dispersed, and countless works of art leaving Venice forever. Determined to preserve his city's heritage, he spent much of his life collecting paintings, maps, manuscripts, coins, sculptures, scientific instruments, and everyday objects that together told the story of Venice.

When Correr died in 1830, he bequeathed his entire collection to the city, together with the wish that it should remain accessible to both scholars and the public. That remarkable gift became the foundation of today's Museo Correr and, ultimately, the wider network of Venice's Civic Museums. Looking around the galleries, it was hard not to appreciate just how much of Venice's history survives today because one man decided it was worth preserving.

A Museum Inside a Royal Palace

One of the things we hadn't realised before visiting was that Museo Correr is housed inside what was once a royal palace. Until Napoleon conquered Venice in 1797, this side of Piazza San Marco was occupied by the Renaissance Church of San Geminiano, designed by Jacopo Sansovino. Napoleon ordered it demolished to make way for a grand imperial residence that reflected the new political order.

The resulting Ala Napoleonica, or Napoleonic Wing, later served as the Venetian residence of three successive ruling dynasties: first Napoleon's Empire, then the Austrian Habsburgs, and finally the Kings of Italy. As we walked through its halls and decorated rooms, it was striking to realise that these galleries had once been the private apartments of emperors, empresses, and kings. The museum doesn't simply tell Venice's history through its collections—the building itself is part of that story.

Venice Beyond Politics

After spending the morning in the Doge's Palace surrounded by politics, diplomacy, and the machinery of government, the Museo Correr offered a different perspective on Venice. Here the focus shifts away from rulers and councils to the people who built, sailed, traded, invented, and lived in the Republic.

The collections cover almost every aspect of Venetian life: shipbuilding and navigation, trade across the Eastern Mediterranean, coinage and banking, the city's powerful guilds, scientific instruments, maps and exploration, education, Carnival traditions, and everyday domestic life. Together they paint a complete picture of how Venice functioned as one of Europe's most cosmopolitan trading cities, where commerce, craftsmanship, and curiosity shaped daily life for centuries.

Maps and the Venetian World

Long before modern navigation, the Republic depended on accurate charts to connect its trading empire across the Adriatic and the Eastern Mediterranean. Merchants, diplomats, and naval captains all relied on precise geographical knowledge, and Venice became one of Renaissance Europe's leading centres of cartography. Looking at the globes, sea charts, and engraved maps, it was easy to appreciate just how much of the Republic's success depended on understanding the world beyond the lagoon.

The highlight was Jacopo de' Barbari's extraordinary View of Venice from 1500. It's much more than a beautiful bird's-eye illustration; it is an astonishingly accurate survey of the city at the height of its power. Every canal, church, and campanile has been rendered with remarkable precision, creating a snapshot of Renaissance Venice that still rewards close inspection more than five centuries later. I could have spent hours studying it alone.

The Picture Gallery

The picture gallery was another part of the museum that deserved far more time than we could give it. Rather than presenting a handful of famous paintings, it tells the story of how Venetian art evolved from the Middle Ages into the High Renaissance. Walking from room to room, you can follow the gradual shift in colour, perspective, and realism as each generation of artists built upon the last.

The collection includes works by some of the greatest names in Italian art, including Lorenzo Veneziano, Giovanni and Gentile Bellini, Vittore Carpaccio, Antonello da Messina, Cosmè Tura, and Lorenzo Lotto.

Carlo Scarpa’s Museum

The museum itself is remarkable. Between 1957 and 1963, the Venetian architect Carlo Scarpa completely reimagined the picture galleries. Instead of filling rooms with paintings, he carefully considered how each work should be seen, using light, spacing, materials, and sightlines to encourage visitors to slow down and really look.

It's easy to miss because nothing feels overdesigned or attention-seeking. Scarpa's interventions are subtle, almost invisible at times, yet they completely shape the experience of moving through the galleries. Today his work at the Museo Correr is regarded as one of the defining achievements of twentieth-century museum design, and after walking through these rooms, it's not difficult to understand why.

Why the Museo Correr Matters

Looking back, the Doge's Palace and the Museo Correr are two halves of the same story. The Doge's Palace explains how Venice governed its empire; the Correr explains the people who made that empire possible. Here the focus shifts from councils and diplomacy to merchants, craftsmen, explorers, artists, scholars, and the everyday life that allowed the Republic to flourish for more than a thousand years.

Together with the Biblioteca Marciana, the two museums tell a remarkably complete story of Venice. One reveals its political power, another preserves its cultural memory, while the library celebrates its pursuit of knowledge. Visiting all three gave us a much deeper appreciation of the city than expected. By the time we left, Venice no longer felt like a collection of beautiful buildings; it felt like a civilisation.

Finally out from the museums

After leaving the Museo Correr, we wandered with a plan to find a lunch place. A few turns later we stumbled across one of the Venice Biennale exhibitions before deciding it was finally time for a late lunch. We ended up at Ombra del Leone, and it couldn't have been a better choice. From our table, we looked out across the Grand Canal towards Punta della Dogana and the brilliant white façade of Basilica di Santa Maria della Salute, one of those unmistakable Venetian views that never seems to lose its impact.

The afternoon heat had taken away most of our appetite, but once the food arrived we quickly forgot about that. The carbonara was wonderfully rich, the gnocchi every bit as good, and the welcome air conditioning made it an easy place to linger a little longer. Between the excellent lunch and the view across the water, it became one of those unexpectedly memorable stops that you never really plan for but end up remembering long after the trip.

After lunch we continued wandering through the quieter streets until we found ourselves in front of the Ateneo Veneto di Scienze, Lettere ed Arti, just beside La Fenice Opera House. At first glance we were convinced it was another Venetian church, but appearances can be deceptive. Today the building is one of Venice's most important cultural institutions, hosting exhibitions, lectures, concerts, and academic events throughout the year.

The building began life in the late sixteenth century as the Church and Convent of San Fantin, home to the Order of the Servants of Mary. Like many religious buildings in Venice, its role changed dramatically during Napoleon's occupation, when monasteries across the city were dissolved. In 1812 it became the headquarters of the newly established Ateneo Veneto, and for more than two centuries it has welcomed generations of artists, scientists, writers, architects, musicians, and scholars.

The façade is elegant rather than extravagant, blending late Renaissance architecture with subtle early Baroque influences. The symmetry immediately catches your eye, while the white Istrian stone seems to glow in the afternoon sun. Looking higher, three monumental statues stand along the roofline. Christ Resurrected occupies the centre, recognised by the radiant halo behind his head, while saints connected to the church's original dedication stand on either side. Beneath them, the pediment contains a relief of the Crucifixion that naturally draws your gaze upwards.

Natasha Tontey – The Phantom Combatants and the Metabolism of Disobedient Organs

6 May – 25 October 2026

During our visit the Ateneo was hosting Indonesian artist Natasha Tontey's The Phantom Combatants and the Metabolism of Disobedient Organs as part of the 61st Venice Biennale. Commissioned by Amos Rex in Helsinki together with LAS Art Foundation in Berlin, the installation combines immersive video, sculpture, sound, and lighting to tell the story of a female Indonesian resistance fighter from the 1950s while exploring themes of bodily autonomy, Indigenous knowledge, surveillance, and resistance. I always enjoy seeing contemporary art in historic settings, and this felt like a particularly good match—centuries-old Renaissance architecture providing the backdrop for a work looking firmly towards the present.

Following Mozart's Footsteps

Standing in front of the plaque and imagining a fifteen-year-old Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart wandering these very streets during the Carnival of 1771 when Venice was one of Europe's great musical capitals, where opera, church music, and Carnival celebrations filled the city with sound from morning until late into the night.

As the guest of local friends, the young Mozart attended performances, met fellow musicians, and experienced Venice at its most colourful and theatrical. He didn't compose any major works during his stay, but it's difficult to believe the city didn't leave its mark. The masks, canals, churches, and constant music must have made quite an impression on a teenager already recognised as a musical prodigy.

More than two centuries later, Venice quietly commemorated that visit with this modest plaque. It's the sort of detail that's easy to walk past, but once you stop for a moment, it's a wonderful reminder that some of history's greatest figures once wandered these same narrow calli, seeing much the same city that we do today.

The rest of the afternoon was spent doing something every trip to Italy seems to include sooner or later: shopping. We wandered our way through San Marco and into San Polo, browsing small boutiques and artisan shops along the way. By the end of it we'd both found new hats, and Mira discovered a beautiful piece of locally made jewellery that would make its debut at La Fenice on Friday evening. Sometimes the nicest souvenirs aren't the ones you planned to buy—they're the ones that simply find you while you're wandering.

An Italian Ending to the Day

Dinner was completely unplanned, which often seems to be when you find the best places. On the walk back towards P1779 we wandered into Al Bagolo, where a simple meal of pizza and bruschetta turned out to be exactly what we were looking for after another full day of walking.

After a short stop back at the apartment, we headed out once more, this time to Marciano Pub, where we joined the locals to watch France comfortably dispatch Morocco in the World Cup quarter-final.

There was just enough energy left for one final stop before calling it a night. At Al Timon we ordered glasses of local Valpolicella and reflected on everything we'd managed to pack into a single day. It felt like the perfect way to finish—good wine, good company, and the quiet satisfaction that comes from knowing tomorrow would bring another adventure.

Friday · 10 July

Corte Sconta, the Biennale, and a night at La Fenice

← 9 Jul Top 11 Jul →

A late start, and a fair one — the last two days of late nights and uneven sleep had finally come to collect. The morning went to journaling and sorting through photos rather than sightseeing. The Doge's Palace and Correr had gone in deep, honestly in a way that's hard to imagine fully recovering from. There's an overwhelming amount of history, culture, and accumulated human achievement packed into this city built on water — far more than seems reasonable for a place this size. It's clear now why she's called La Serenissima. She really is serene, though not in a quiet sense, but with the calm confidence of a city that has known exactly what it is for centuries.

And it wasn't simply a city where Renaissance masterpieces ended up. Venice actively helped make the Renaissance happen. The Republic's immense trading wealth funded artists and architects much as Florence's banking dynasties did, while Aldus Manutius's printing press transformed learning itself, putting Greek and Latin classics into circulation faster and more affordably than anywhere else in Europe. Italic type, the portable pocket-sized book, the spread of humanist thought — so much of it passed through Venice first.

Cardinal Bessarion entrusted the city with his remarkable collection of Greek manuscripts, rescued as Byzantium fell, and that library became the foundation of the Biblioteca Marciana — the very room with Sansovino's extraordinary ceiling I'd stood beneath the day before. On Murano, Venetian glassmakers pushed their craft to heights unmatched anywhere else, while painters like Bellini, Titian, and Veronese benefited from pigments and techniques that gave Venetian art its unmistakable richness and light.

Perhaps most importantly, Venice occupied a unique position between Byzantium, the Islamic world, and Latin Europe. Long before anyone spoke of a "Renaissance," ideas flowed into the city alongside silk, spices, and precious goods. Walking through Correr and the Marciana, it became obvious that they weren't simply monuments to wealth collected from elsewhere. They were monuments to what Venice itself had created.

The walk toward Castello was hot under the midday sun, but for once mercifully uncrowded. Campo after campo lay almost empty, the canals and bridges catching the light in that flickering, liquid way Venice does when there isn't another soul in the frame.

Corte Sconta

Lunch was the set menu at Corte Sconta, tucked away in a vine-covered courtyard warm enough to test Finnish enthusiasm for eating outdoors — though eating outside won, as it usually does. Mira went for the black squid-ink pasta, expecting something she'd have to learn to appreciate. I ordered mussels with chanterelles. Halfway through we swapped plates, and somewhere between the first and second bite the squid ink quietly won me over. One of those dishes that turns out to be far gentler, richer, and more addictive than its appearance suggests.

The Biennale

The Biennale Arte at the Arsenale ran darker than expected — a curatorial mood that seemed to track a genuinely uneasy present: a world at war in places, a hard-right isolationist current rising in others, conservatism seasoning all of it. The Arsenale itself is enormous, and knowing it once ran as the industrial engine behind Venetian naval power centuries ago reframes the whole visit — oceans rise, empires fall, and presumably someone, someday, will look back at this exact moment the same way.

Exhaustion won out over more walking, so the trip back was by vaporetto — Line 1 from Arsenale up toward Ca' Rezzonico, which turned out to be every bit as fun as a gondola ride and considerably cheaper per minute. The 24-hour ticket bought that morning would also cover the run out to Piazzale Roma the next day, which felt like a small win.

Back to P 1779 to change, then a quick pasta dinner at La Corte 1642 — a carbonara good enough that Mikael is already planning a return visit purely to order it again.

The Line 2 vaporetto to Rialto came with an unplanned obstacle: the Grand Canal was flooding at the landing, which meant shoes off, trousers rolled up, and a wade to the platform to make the boat in time. Lesson filed away for future acqua alta: shoes go back on before boarding — bare feet are apparently not a welcome sight to Venetian vaporetto crews.

La Fenice was worth every bit of the wade. The interior is somehow ornate to the point of looking edible — a cream-cake of a room — without ever tipping into too much. Cornelius Meister conducted a late-Romantic program: Strauss's Don Juan, Dvořák's The Midday Witch (Polednice), and Brahms's Symphony No. 2. The orchestra was genuinely world-class, and the sound held up even from the far back-left of the floor.

It turned out to be Meister's official debut with the La Fenice orchestra, which makes the pairing read less like a random program and more like a statement — one that got its own write-up rather than crowding the day's entry: Concert Notes, on the Notes page.

The name earns its keep. La Fenice means "the Phoenix," and the theater has lived up to it three separate times — burning down and rebuilding in 1774, 1836, and again in 1996, each time rising back into more or less the same late-Baroque splendor: red velvet, heavy gold leaf, the kind of ornate ceiling work that makes a room feel gilded rather than just decorated.

It's acoustically one of the most highly regarded halls in Europe, which tracked with how the sound carried even from a cheap seat on the floor. And it earned its reputation the hard way, in the 19th century, as a genuine center of Italian opera — Verdi premiered both Rigoletto and La Traviata here, on this exact stage, long before it burned for the second and third time.

The program itself turned out to be a smart bit of curation, not just a nice-sounding trio of pieces. This was Cornelius Meister's official debut with the La Fenice orchestra, and Strauss, Dvořák, and Brahms sit on opposite sides of a real 19th-century fault line: Strauss and Dvořák belonged to the camp that believed music should tell a literal story — Don Juan is a specific lover's tale in sound, The Midday Witch tracks an actual folk-horror plot beat by beat — while Brahms represented the other school, the one that trusted pure musical form over any attached narrative.

Placed on the same program, the split collapses a little: all three composers were reaching for the same scale of emotional weight, just arguing about how honest to be about the story behind it. There's a personal thread running underneath, too — Brahms was the one who championed Dvořák early and got him published internationally, and a young Strauss studied Brahms's orchestration closely before breaking off in his own more radical direction.

And practically speaking, the three pieces cover the whole orchestra: Don Juan demands speed and precision, especially from the horns; the Dvořák leans on the woodwinds for its eerie theatrical effects; the Brahms needs nothing but pure, unified string tone and control. A genuinely well-chosen way for a new conductor to introduce himself to an orchestra — and to a hall that's heard a great many debuts before this one.

The vaporetto back from Rialto afterward came with the first real flicker of feeling like a local rather than a visitor — a nice note to end on, walking back past the winged lion at Campo Manin in the blue-hour dark. And, fittingly for a football-inflected trip, they made it back in time to catch Spain's second goal against Belgium in the World Cup quarter-final. A fine day around the islands, all told.

Saturday · 11 July

Venice to the Riviera del Brenta

← 10 Jul Top 12 Jul →

Live journaling today, more or less as it happens. Left Venice by ATVO bus and picked up the rental car at Marco Polo — the booked Mercedes-class wasn't available, so the consolation prize was a BMW X1 instead. Black, at least, which around here counts as a small mercy: nobody's roasting alive in a black car in July, they just look moderately more respectable doing it.

First stop was the town of Mira on the Riviera del Brenta, for lunch at the local cicchetteria, A Na' Certa — bread, cured meats, a sliced local cheese, a bit of tartare, and a fritter or two, the small-plates format doing exactly what it's meant to. It also produced the trip's first bottle of wine: a local Valpolicella Superiore for €14, which felt like a genuine bargain given that a Superiore back home at the Finnish Alko runs well north of €20.

A second pit stop followed at the Ipercity in Albignasego — less romantic than a Palladian villa, but necessary: socks and underwear restocked, and an extra piece of luggage picked up to get the growing wine collection and everything else home in one piece.

From Mira it was on through the winding hill roads around Valsanzibio — vineyards on both sides most of the way — to Le Volpi, tucked into the Euganean Hills. Fitting, given the name: "le volpi" means "the foxes," and sure enough there's a pair of them keeping watch over the place.

After settling in, the day wasn't quite done — a drive back out to Padova for pizza. Worth it for more than the food: a golden-hour shot of weathered statues against a glowing sky (the kind of light that makes even a stray TV antenna look intentional), and a stencil piece of street art — a boy watering a row of hearts instead of flowers — tucked into a wall along the way.

Pizza itself delivered — two full-sized pies, no regrets about portion sizes. Then back to Le Volpi in time to catch Norway play England, closing out a day that started with a wrong-class rental car and ended with vineyards, foxes, and football.

Sunday · 12 July

Down from the hills to Padova

← 11 Jul Top 13 Jul →

Waking up to a new day in a winery here in the hills is something else. The breakfast at Le Volpi was lovely, and the view nothing less than stunning.

Sunday's plan changed course: Padova instead of Arquà Petrarca. The rental car handled the hills better than expected, and the drive down wasn't nearly as bad as feared — plus the city had its own claim on the day regardless, with the university that hosted Mira for a semester meriting a proper, thorough visit.

The drive down came with its own experience: a steady scatter of bicyclists along the winding road through Fontanafredda, Galzignano Terme, and Torreglia — proof, once again, that everyone behind a wheel in these hills fancies themselves a Formula 1 driver working out an overtake.

The university itself did not disappoint. Palazzo del Bo — Padova's ancient seat of learning, founded in 1222 and one of the oldest universities in the world — greeted us through a stone archway with the EU, Italian, and Veneto flags overhead and Universitas degli Studi carved above the door. Galileo taught here for the better part of two decades; walking through the same gate felt like a different register of "old" than even Venice manages.

Inside, a frescoed staircase climbs toward the Rector's office — the sign at the top reads simply AL RETTORE — walls covered floor to ceiling in painted scholars, saints, and allegorical figures, the whole stairwell doubling as a history lesson on the way up.

Coffee afterward at Caffè Pedrocchi, the grand neoclassical café a short walk from the university — historically nicknamed "the café without doors." For decades after it opened in 1831 it genuinely had none, staying open around the clock for students, revolutionaries, and anyone else who wandered in off the street.

In Piazza dei Signori, the Torre dell'Orologio's astronomical clock turned into its own small puzzle — one of the oldest astronomical clocks in the world, dating to 1344, its blue-and-gold dial running the zodiac around a starry field. Except count the signs and one is conspicuously missing: Libra, dropped from the wheel — a holdover from antiquity, when the scales were folded into Scorpio's claws rather than treated as a sign of their own.

The scales turned up anyway, a few steps away on the flank of the Palazzo della Ragione — the enormous medieval hall known locally as Il Salone, its barrel-vaulted roof one of the largest unsupported roofs in Europe. A sundial mounted on the brickwork carries its own set of zodiac symbols, Libra included this time, closing the loop the clock tower left open.

The day closed at the Basilica di Sant'Antonio, known throughout Padova simply as Il Santo — a genuinely massive pile of brick and lead-grey domes, a silhouette owing more to Byzantium than to anything else on this trip so far, holding the tomb of St. Anthony of Padua at its center.

Prato della Valle itself, in the end, got only a supporting role: lunch stayed simple — a glass of fresh orange juice back at Pedrocchi, then pizza and salami picked up from the Despar next to the Prato della Valle parking — before pointing the car back up into the hills to Le Volpi.

Monte Fasolo sits close enough to Le Volpi that walking up seemed like a reasonable idea. It wasn't. The scorching heat and the rough dirt road would have called for proper hiking gear rather than our sandals and light sneakers, so we gave up on the sports part and hopped into the now-trusty diesel-powered Beamer instead.

It turned out we were the only ones at the winery — so we got a private tour of the cellars, the production facilities, and the tasting. The estate covers over 270 hectares, of which 110 are under vine and the rest given over to olive groves and other agricultural use. They grow several different grape varieties across many lines of wines, and the scale shows: tens of thousands of bottles a year, a seriously big producer for the area. The facilities are strikingly modern — the latest technology monitoring the wines resting in concrete storage, and oak barrels of equal quality alongside. The Rossi Luciani family owns both Monte Fasolo and Le Volpi, and has brought them back to life from neglect.

The tasting ran spumante, bianco, and two rossi, all from the Linea di Gi: the Fior d'Ape Metodo Classico — Vino Spumante di Qualità, Moscato Giallo 100%, Millesimato Extra Brut; the Àrio Colli Euganei DOC, Manzoni Bianco 100%; the 12 Mesi Colli Euganei DOC, Merlot 100%; and the 24 Mesi Colli Euganei DOC Rosso — Merlot 60%, Cabernet Sauvignon 30%, Cabernet Franc 10%.

The sparkling is nothing like sweet proseccos — made metodo classico, it's crisp, dry, and expressive. The white was floral and light, a spring breeze almost. Moving to the reds, the profile shifted to darker flowers, with passion and intensity, but still floral underneath. The reds are very modern too, and incredibly versatile — but from Monte Fasolo it's the sparklings that are the most interesting bottles.

After the tasting we took a stroll around the garden and had a look at the vines on top of the hill — they grow so tall here in Italy. The place carries a long history too: the old Oratory of San Gaetano da Thiene still stands, a reminder of the time when there was a monastery on the site.

By the time we came back down from the vineyards, the restaurant had opened — families, couples, and groups of friends filling the patio for dinner. We stayed, and put the sommelier's suggestions to work: the Fior d'Ape metodo classico with the antipasti (uovo poché for Mira, a tartare for me), then bigoli al ragù di corte with the 8 Mesi Cabernet for Mira, and costine di maiale — glazed with mustard and honey — with the Carmenère for me. The pairings were good: the Carmenère really fought off the pork fat, and the lighter 8 Mesi brought the light meat to life. We returned to Le Volpi with olive oil, spumante, and a bottle of the 24 Mesi. Overall, an amazing day.

Monday · 13 July

Monselice market and the Vignalta terroir

← 12 Jul Top 14 Jul →

Woke up to a fresh new morning and this amazing view — the citrus tree, a barrel by the railing, and the whole plain unrolling far below the terrace.

We decided on a quick drive down to check the market at Monselice. Another hot day, and our feet are aching after so many days of walking — first Venice, then yesterday Padova. A gelato in town, then back to Le Volpi for a cold plate for lunch.

Then off to Vignalta. The day was hot again, as we've now grown accustomed to in Veneto, and there was no discussion about whether we walk or drive. For some reason there seem to be no other people having tastings in the Colli Euganei just now, so we were treated to another private tour and tasting. A serious and quiet man called Michele took us in and started the tour with a quick look at the fields, to get an understanding of the terroir the vines grow in — then swiftly back inside, as the scorching afternoon sun was intense.

Vignalta's facilities are smaller than Monte Fasolo's, but they still produce a decent yield. Interestingly, they have left a wall of bare bedrock open in their new barrel storage, to visualize where the vines are extracting their flavors. Michele said the deepest roots reach ten meters down into the trachyte and limestone layers — incredible how strong the roots are. He also told us that the stone paving Piazza San Marco in Venice is Euganean trachyte.

The tasting normally runs sparkling, white, red, and a dessert wine — but now that we were alone, Michele let us pick the ones we wanted, so we switched the dessert for the Carmenère. What followed turned out to be a real introduction class to Vignalta's wines.

The sparkling was the Blanc de Noirs Brut Nature — Raboso Friularo — a wild and free sparkling, bubbles of wild northern acidic apples; a wine I'd pair with anchovy cicchetti. It was followed by the Gemola Chardonnay, a fun, creamy, easy-to-drink white. I made a fun little observation that this one was somehow American in profile — and Michele didn't just confirm it, he answered by producing the actual bottle from the shelf: a Mount Eden Vineyards Santa Cruz Mountains Chardonnay 2020. Lucio lives part-time in California, he explained, and it was there that Mount Eden inspired him to make something similar at Vignalta.

We moved on to the Carmenère, aged 24 months in French oak tonneaux, and this one was a party in the mouth — spicy and joyful, but with the elegance that seems to be the base note in every bottle here. Michele then pulled an everyday wine, the Rosso Riserva 2020 (Merlot–Cabernet): excellent, deep and serious but easy to drink. Followed by the signature bottle, the Gemola 2019 — Merlot 70%, Cabernet Franc 30%. The seriousness took a few steps up; now we're talking Christmas-table wine.

Somewhere in the discussions on grape varieties and taste profiles — and how much these reds resemble Bordeaux — Michele let us have an extra glass of the Cabernet Riserva 2011, 100% Cabernet Sauvignon, made simply because it was possible. This is not a wine for making money; this is a wine to keep and let develop.

One fascinating detail emerged along the way: the quiet, serious Michele guiding us turned out to be Michele Montecchio — the head winemaker of the whole show. No wonder the introduction class was this good; we'd had the professor himself.

We wandered off from the tasting with a bottle of the Carmenère and the Cab 2011 — the ones I described as the one to go to a game with, and the one to go home to for deep discussions.

The day finished with a dip in Le Volpi's infinity pool and a quick run to Arquà Petrarca, to Osteria al Guerriero. Lasagne paired with an Alla Costiera Biancone, and my anchovy pasta with the Alberto metodo classico. All in all, a good day.

Tuesday · 14 July

The last day: records, one more Padova, and home

← 13 Jul Top

The last day in Italy — there is no way to write that sentence that doesn't sting a little. One more breakfast on the Le Volpi terrace, then the final packing, which had become a ritual of its own: every bottle swaddled in shirts and cinched tight, a suitcase built around wine with some clothes along for the ride.

With the flight not leaving until 19:15, there was time for one last visit to Padova. Checkout, and down from the hills a final time — the winding road all ours now, the weekend's bicyclists gone back to their weekday lives.

In Padova we ducked into a record store and came out heavier: a compilation of Italian classics — La Canzone Italiana: In Giro per l'Italia — a Peppino di Capri album (…è mezzanotte), and the soundtrack of The Godfather Part II. After a light pizza lunch we went looking for an older chapter of this story: Mira's student dwelling near the Chiesa di San Pio X on Via Paolo Fambri, still standing exactly where she left it.

There was still plenty of time, so we took the slow way to the aeroporto, stopping for a slushy in Noale. The town kept its cards close — the Rocca was closed, so we admired it over the moat — but the piazza offered the trusty Beamer one last photogenic posting beneath the tower gate.

The final refuel came to €52 of diesel — a more than fair tab for a week of hauling us, our wine, and our ambitions up and down the Euganean Hills. Drop-off was painless, the same spot we'd collected it from, and the attendant was friendly in that unhurried Italian way: before the keys had even changed hands he was telling us about his nephew's winery in the Colli Euganei, for when we come back. When. Not if.

We had budgeted generously for the drop-off and spent the surplus waiting for the bag drop to open. From there, the business upgrade earned its keep twice over: a hefty security queue skipped in a stride, and a delayed flight waited out in the lounge, the lagoon shimmering on the far side of the glass.

The flight itself passed smoothly, the service up front feeling more like a last course than a commute — a worthy end to the trip. We landed into a Helsinki dusk that never quite committed to darkness, two suitcases heavier and already talking about when.

Buon viaggio — Venice to the vineyards.
