Wednesday · 8 July

Arrival, and the first spritz

Up at 5am with the kind of groggy dread only a 05:35 Bolt pickup can produce. By the time we were blinking in the queue at HEL, half the terminal seemed to be Japanese tour groups also transferring through — a very on-brand start to a trip about wandering with the crowds instead of against them.

AY1781 left on time. Marco Polo greeted us with the usual arrivals-hall theatre: hunting down the ATVO counter for return bus tickets while trying hard not to be mistaken for the Delta charter's worth of Americans fanning out around us. Lane D2, tickets in hand, off to Piazzale Roma.

First stop in the city proper: bruschette and cappuccini at La Lista near Campo San Geremia — the correct way to arrive in Venice, sitting still with coffee before the legs take over. Then P 1779, where Max's local tips turned out to be the real welcome packet: where to eat, where to drink, where to shoot the good photos, where to actually shop instead of just window-gawking.

From there we did what Venice demands on a first afternoon — wandered without much of a plan, landed at San Sofia for the obligatory spritz, watched the light do its thing over the canal. Back to P 1779 to change, then out again through the Ghetto Nuovo, that quieter corner of Cannaregio with its tall houses and its own particular hush.

Dinner was Osteria Anice Stellato, and the wine list did more work than the plates — which is a real compliment given how good the antipasti was. Lambrusco to open alongside smoked tomato and anchovy; green snail gnocchi with a Sassi di Matera red; mussel spaghettone with a crisp Verdicchio mescita. Antipasti-forward, wine-led, exactly right for night one.

Thursday · 9 July

Doges, Etruscans, and a French rout

Groggy start — the early Wednesday wakeup finally caught up with us. Breakfast in the courtyard made up for it, wasps circling the jam notwithstanding: eggs, cured meats, cheese, fruit, two cappuccini apiece worth of resolve for the day ahead.

Then a quick march through Cannaregio into San Marco, racing the clock. We'd budgeted the Doge's Palace first — facade glowing white-and-pink in the sun, the Bridge of Sighs and prison wing squeezed in at the end so we wouldn't miss our 11:30 slot at Museo Correr. Mikael took an unreasonable number of ceiling photos inside; no regrets.

Correr turned out to be the real surprise of the day — an exhibition arcing from the Etruscans clear through to Napoleon, by way of antique atlases, a Fra Mauro-style world map under glass, a wall-sized 16th-century view of the Basilica on fire, and — improbably — a cabinet of ammonites. The kind of eclectic civic museum that rewards wandering more than checklist-ticking.

Late lunch at Ombra del Leone, with the Punta della Dogana and the white flank of the Salute basilica sitting across the water like part of the menu. The afternoon dissolved into shopping through San Marco and San Polo — hats for both of us, and a piece of locally made jewelry for Mira to wear to La Fenice on Friday.

Dinner was unplanned and better for it: pizza and bruschetta at Al Bagolo, wandered into on the way home. A stop at P 1779, then out again to Marciano Pub to watch France dispatch Morocco with ease in the World Cup quarter-final. Closed the night with a glass of local Valpolicella at Al Timon — the correct way to end a very full day.

Buon viaggio — Venice to the vineyards.
