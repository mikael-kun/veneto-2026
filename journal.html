Wednesday · 8 July

Arrival, and the first spritz

Top 9 Jul →

Up at 5am with the kind of groggy dread only a 05:35 Bolt pickup can produce — the alarm less a sound than an accusation. By the time we stood blinking in the queue at HEL, half the terminal seemed to be Japanese tour groups also transferring through, and it felt like a very on-brand start to a trip about wandering with the crowds instead of against them.

AY1781 left on time. Marco Polo greeted us with the usual arrivals-hall theatre: hunting down the ATVO counter for return bus tickets while trying hard not to be mistaken for the Delta charter's worth of Americans fanning out around us. Lane D2, tickets in hand, and the bus rolled us across the water to Piazzale Roma.

First stop in the city proper: bruschette and cappuccini at La Lista near Campo San Geremia — the correct way to arrive in Venice, sitting still with coffee while the city starts moving around you, before the legs take over. Then P 1779, where Max's local tips turned out to be the real welcome packet: where to eat, where to drink, where to shoot the good photos, where to actually shop instead of just window-gawking.

From there we did what Venice demands of a first afternoon — wandered without much of a plan, let the calli make the decisions, and landed at San Sofia for the obligatory spritz, watching the light do its slow theatre over the canal. Back to P 1779 to change, then out again through the Ghetto Nuovo, that quieter corner of Cannaregio where the houses grow tall and the noise falls away into its own particular hush.

Dinner was Osteria Anice Stellato, and the wine list did more work than the plates — a real compliment, given how good the antipasti was. Lambrusco to open, against smoked tomato and anchovy; green snail gnocchi with a Sassi di Matera red; mussel spaghettone with a crisp Verdicchio mescita. Antipasti-forward, wine-led, exactly right for night one.

Thursday · 9 July

Doges, Etruscans, and a French rout

← 8 Jul Top 10 Jul →

Groggy start — the early Wednesday wakeup finally caught up with us. Breakfast in the courtyard made up for it, wasps circling the jam notwithstanding: eggs, cured meats, cheese, fruit, two cappuccini apiece worth of resolve for the day ahead.

Then a quick march through Cannaregio into San Marco, racing the clock. We'd budgeted the Doge's Palace first — facade glowing white-and-pink in the sun, the Bridge of Sighs and prison wing squeezed in at the end so we wouldn't miss our 11:30 slot at Museo Correr. Mikael took an unreasonable number of ceiling photos inside; no regrets.

Correr turned out to be the real surprise of the day — an exhibition arcing from the Etruscans clear through to Napoleon, by way of antique atlases, the actual Fra Mauro world map under glass, a wall-sized 16th-century view of the Basilica on fire, and — improbably — a cabinet of ammonites. The kind of eclectic civic museum that rewards wandering more than checklist-ticking. The coin cabinets, the Sansovino Library ceiling, and the Fra Mauro map turned into their own rabbit hole — written up separately on the Notes page.

Bocca di Leone (Lion’s Mouth Complaint Box)

This stone letterbox was used to receive written denunciations during the Venetian Republic. Citizens could anonymously report suspected crimes such as tax fraud, corruption, or officials concealing public revenue. While often described as “anonymous accusation boxes,” not every complaint was accepted—serious accusations generally required supporting evidence before authorities would investigate.

Caught a view into inaccessible section of the Doge’s Palace. Beyond the Gothic loggia, the Italian flag overlooks the lagoon toward San Giorgio Maggiore — a reminder that this historic palace is not only a museum but also a living institution, with parts of the building still reserved for official cultural and administrative use.

Scala d’Oro (Golden Staircase)

The Golden Staircase was the ceremonial entrance to the Doge’s apartments and the chambers of the Venetian government. Designed by Jacopo Sansovino and completed by Antonio Scarpagnino in 1559, its lavish stucco decoration by Alessandro Vittoria and frescoes by Giambattista Franco transformed a simple staircase into a statement of power. Every mythological figure reinforces Venice’s image as a wise, prosperous, and divinely favored maritime republic.

Why it mattered

Unlike modern government buildings, Renaissance Venice deliberately used architecture as political messaging. Anyone invited to climb the Scala d’Oro—foreign ambassadors, nobles, or high-ranking officials—would ascend beneath a ceiling proclaiming the Republic’s wealth, classical learning, military strength, and naval supremacy before ever reaching the Doge’s chambers.

Sala delle Quattro Porte (Room of the Four Doors)

Rebuilt after the fire of 1574 to designs by Andrea Palladio and Giovanni Antonio Rusconi, the Room of the Four Doors served as the formal antechamber to the Republic’s principal council rooms. Ambassadors, envoys, and delegations waited here before being received — which makes the decoration less ornament than argument, put to work on visitors during the one stretch of the day when they had nothing to do but look.

The ceiling

The ceiling itself reinforces Venice’s political message. Tintoretto’s mythological paintings depict Venice under the protection and approval of the classical gods. Rather than merely decorating the room, the artwork presents the Republic as a state founded on wisdom, justice, and divine favor—a recurring theme throughout the Doge’s Palace.

The four doors

The room takes its name from the four monumental doorways that give onto it, each framed in precious marbles and crowned with allegorical sculpture. They open toward the chambers where the Republic’s business was actually conducted, which turned a single waiting room into a hinge of Venetian government — the door that opened told you which body had decided to see you.

Sala del Senato (Senate Chamber)

The Senate Chamber was where the Venetian Senate met to direct the Republic’s foreign policy, trade, naval affairs, and finances. Composed of experienced patricians, the Senate governed Venice’s commercial empire and made many of the decisions that shaped its influence across the Mediterranean. The lavish decoration by Tintoretto and other Renaissance masters transforms the chamber into a celebration of wise government, divine guidance, and the enduring strength of the Republic.

Sala del Maggior Consiglio (Chamber of the Great Council)

At 53 metres long and 25 metres wide, the Chamber of the Great Council is one of Europe’s largest historic council halls. Here, more than a thousand Venetian nobles gathered to elect officials, approve laws, and shape the future of the Republic. Rebuilt after the great fire of 1577, the hall is surrounded by monumental canvases celebrating Venice’s history and crowned by Tintoretto’s Paradise—one of the largest oil paintings ever created.

Ponte dei Sospiri (The Bridge of Sighs)

Completed in 1614, the Bridge of Sighs was built to connect the Doge’s Palace with the New Prisons (Prigioni Nuove) across the Rio di Palazzo. Despite its graceful Baroque exterior, the enclosed bridge served a purely practical purpose: moving prisoners securely between the courtrooms and their cells.

Inside, the bridge contains two separate corridors divided by a wall. One carried prisoners between the courtrooms of the Doge’s Palace and the prisons, while the other connected the prisons with the offices of the State Advocates. Both corridors also linked to stairways leading to the lower cells (Pozzi) and the attic prisons (Piombi).

The famous name “Bridge of Sighs” is actually much younger than the bridge itself. It became popular during the Romantic period, inspired by the idea that condemned prisoners would sigh as they caught their final glimpse of Venice through the small stone lattice windows before beginning their sentence. In reality, many prisoners saw only narrow views of canals rather than the sweeping panoramas imagined by poets.

San Marco and transition to Museo Correr

Out of the palace and straight into the glare — from the cool of the Maggior Consiglio into midday San Marco at its most San Marco. The Campanile throwing its one hard shadow, Sansovino's Loggetta blinding white at the foot of it, and the whole square moving at once in every direction. Correr sits at the far end, folded into the Ala Napoleonica that closes the piazza, which means getting there is a crossing rather than a walk.

Which turned out to be the joke of the place. - wading through the crowd at ground level, up the stairs, and the first thing the museum hands back is the piazza itself — the same square, the same crowd, flattened out below the window with the Basilica's domes and the Campanile stacked behind. Worth the crossing before a single exhibit.

Museo Correr

Correr is not just an art museum, it tells the story of Venice from antiquity through the Republic, Napoleon, and the Habsburg period.

The Origins of the Museum

The Museo Correr owes its existence to Teodoro Correr (1750–1830), one of the last great Venetian patricians. Living through the collapse of the Republic in 1797, Correr witnessed churches being suppressed, noble collections dispersed and centuries of Venetian heritage sold across Europe. Rather than allowing these objects to disappear, he devoted his life to collecting paintings, maps, manuscripts, coins, sculpture, scientific instruments and everyday artefacts.

When he died in 1830, Correr left not only his collection to the city but also detailed instructions that it should remain accessible to scholars and to the public. His vision created what would become the nucleus of today’s network of Venetian Civic Museums.

A Museum Inside a Royal Palace

The museum occupies one of the most symbolic buildings on Piazza San Marco.

Until Napoleon conquered Venice in 1797, this side of the square was occupied by the Renaissance church of San Geminiano, designed by Jacopo Sansovino. Napoleon ordered the church demolished to create a grand imperial palace worthy of French rule.

The resulting Napoleonic Wing (Ala Napoleonica) later became the Venetian residence of three successive dynasties:

  • Napoleon’s Empire
  • the Austrian Habsburgs
  • the Kings of Italy

Walking through the museum therefore means walking through rooms that once housed emperors, empresses and kings.

Venice Beyond Politics

Unlike the Doge’s Palace, the Correr explores ordinary Venetian civilisation.

Its collections document:

  • navigation and shipbuilding
  • trade with the Eastern Mediterranean
  • coinage and banking
  • guilds and crafts
  • scientific instruments
  • festivals and Carnival
  • domestic life
  • education
  • maps and exploration

Together these collections reveal how Venice functioned as one of Europe’s most cosmopolitan trading cities for centuries.

Maps and the Venetian World

Venice ruled not through vast land armies but through the sea. Its merchants, diplomats and captains required exceptionally accurate charts and geographical knowledge. The museum’s globes, maps and engravings demonstrate Venice’s role as one of Europe’s leading centres of cartography during the Renaissance. Perhaps the most celebrated image is Jacopo de’ Barbari’s monumental View of Venice (1500), an astonishing bird’s-eye map that combines artistic beauty with remarkable surveying precision, documenting the city at the height of its power.

⸻

The Picture Gallery

The museum’s picture gallery traces the birth of Venetian painting from the Middle Ages through the High Renaissance.

Visitors encounter masterpieces by artists including:

  • Lorenzo Veneziano
  • Giovanni Bellini
  • Gentile Bellini
  • Vittore Carpaccio
  • Antonello da Messina
  • Cosmè Tura
  • Lorenzo Lotto

The galleries were redesigned in the 1950s by renowned architect Carlo Scarpa, whose understated modern displays remain celebrated examples of twentieth-century museum design.

⸻

Carlo Scarpa’s Museum

One of the museum’s hidden masterpieces is not an artwork but the museum itself. Between 1957 and 1963, Venetian architect Carlo Scarpa transformed the picture galleries with a revolutionary exhibition design. Rather than filling rooms with paintings, he carefully controlled light, sightlines, spacing and materials so that each artwork could be appreciated individually. His interventions—minimal yet exquisitely detailed—became hugely influential in modern museum architecture and are studied around the world.

⸻

Why the Museo Correr Matters

If the Doge’s Palace represents the power of Venice, the Museo Correr represents its memory. Here, political history gives way to cultural history. Rather than celebrating rulers alone, the museum reveals the people, ideas, craftsmanship and artistic achievements that sustained the Republic for over a millennium. Together with the Doge’s Palace and the Biblioteca Marciana, the Museo Correr completes one of Europe’s most comprehensive narratives of a city’s rise, transformation and enduring cultural legacy.

Late lunch at Ombra del Leone, with the Punta della Dogana and the white flank of the Salute basilica sitting across the water like part of the menu.

The afternoon dissolved into shopping through San Marco and San Polo — hats for both of us, and a piece of locally made jewelry for Mira to wear to La Fenice on Friday.

Dinner was unplanned and better for it: pizza and bruschetta at Al Bagolo, wandered into on the way home. A stop at P 1779, then out again to Marciano Pub to watch France dispatch Morocco with ease in the World Cup quarter-final. Closed the night with a glass of local Valpolicella at Al Timon — the correct way to end a very full day.

Friday · 10 July

Corte Sconta, the Biennale, and a night at La Fenice

← 9 Jul Top 11 Jul →

A late start, and a fair one — the last two days' worth of late nights and uneven sleep finally sent the bill. The morning went to journaling and sorting through photos rather than sightseeing. The Doge's Palace and Correr had gone in deep — pierced straight through, honestly, in a way that's hard to imagine fully recovering from. There's an overwhelming amount of history, culture, and accumulated human wealth packed into this city built on water, more than feels reasonable for a place this size. It's clear now why she's called La Serenissima — she really is serene, in that particular way only a city this old and this sure of itself can be.

And it wasn't just accumulated wealth sitting still — Venice actively made the Renaissance happen, not merely hosted it. The Republic's trade fortune bankrolled artists and architects the way Florence's banking families did; its printing presses, Aldus Manutius's above all, put Greek and Latin classics into circulation faster and cheaper than anywhere else in Europe, inventing italic type and the modern pocket-sized book along the way.

Cardinal Bessarion handed the city his library of Greek manuscripts, rescued out of a falling Byzantium, and that collection became the seed of the Biblioteca Marciana — the very room with the Sansovino ceiling from the day before. Venetian glassmakers on Murano and colorists in every workshop in the city gave Titian, Bellini, and Veronese pigments no one else had access to.

And sitting where it does, at the hinge between Byzantium, the Islamic world, and Latin Europe, Venice was importing ideas along with silk and spice centuries before anyone used the word "Renaissance" for what was happening. Correr and the Marciana ceiling weren't just showing off what Venice bought — they were showing what Venice built.

The walk toward Castello was hot at midday, but for once mercifully uncrowded — campo after campo nearly empty, the canals and bridges doing that flickering-light thing Venice does when there's no one else in the frame.

Lunch was the set menu at Corte Sconta, in a vine-covered courtyard hot enough to test Finnish enthusiasm for eating outdoors — though eating outdoors won, as it generally does. Mira went for the black squid-ink pasta, an acquired taste she was bracing for; Mikael ordered mussels and chanterelles. They ended up swapping halfway through, and the squid ink won a convert on the spot.

The Biennale Arte at the Arsenale ran darker than expected — a curatorial mood that seemed to track a genuinely uneasy present: a world at war in places, a hard-right isolationist current rising in others, conservatism seasoning all of it. The Arsenale itself is enormous, and knowing it once ran as the industrial engine behind Venetian naval power centuries ago reframes the whole visit — oceans rise, empires fall, and presumably someone, someday, will look back at this exact moment the same way.

Exhaustion won out over more walking, so the trip back was by vaporetto — Line 1 from Arsenale up toward Ca' Rezzonico, which turned out to be every bit as fun as a gondola ride and considerably cheaper per minute. The 24-hour ticket bought that morning would also cover the run out to Piazzale Roma the next day, which felt like a small win.

Back to P 1779 to change, then a quick pasta dinner at La Corte 1642 — a carbonara good enough that Mikael is already planning a return visit purely to order it again.

The Line 2 vaporetto to Rialto came with an unplanned obstacle: the Grand Canal was flooding at the landing, which meant shoes off, trousers rolled up, and a wade to the platform to make the boat in time. Lesson filed away for future acqua alta: shoes go back on before boarding — bare feet are apparently not a welcome sight to Venetian vaporetto crews.

La Fenice was worth every bit of the wade. The interior is somehow ornate to the point of looking edible — a cream-cake of a room — without ever tipping into too much. Cornelius Meister conducted a late-Romantic program: Strauss's Don Juan, Dvořák's The Midday Witch (Polednice), and Brahms's Symphony No. 2. The orchestra was genuinely world-class, and the sound held up even from the far back-left of the floor.

It turned out to be Meister's official debut with the La Fenice orchestra, which makes the pairing read less like a random program and more like a statement — one that got its own write-up rather than crowding the day's entry: Concert Notes, on the Notes page.

The name earns its keep. La Fenice means "the Phoenix," and the theater has lived up to it three separate times — burning down and rebuilding in 1774, 1836, and again in 1996, each time rising back into more or less the same late-Baroque splendor: red velvet, heavy gold leaf, the kind of ornate ceiling work that makes a room feel gilded rather than just decorated.

It's acoustically one of the most highly regarded halls in Europe, which tracked with how the sound carried even from a cheap seat on the floor. And it earned its reputation the hard way, in the 19th century, as a genuine center of Italian opera — Verdi premiered both Rigoletto and La Traviata here, on this exact stage, long before it burned for the second and third time.

The program itself turned out to be a smart bit of curation, not just a nice-sounding trio of pieces. This was Cornelius Meister's official debut with the La Fenice orchestra, and Strauss, Dvořák, and Brahms sit on opposite sides of a real 19th-century fault line: Strauss and Dvořák belonged to the camp that believed music should tell a literal story — Don Juan is a specific lover's tale in sound, The Midday Witch tracks an actual folk-horror plot beat by beat — while Brahms represented the other school, the one that trusted pure musical form over any attached narrative.

Placed on the same program, the split collapses a little: all three composers were reaching for the same scale of emotional weight, just arguing about how honest to be about the story behind it. There's a personal thread running underneath, too — Brahms was the one who championed Dvořák early and got him published internationally, and a young Strauss studied Brahms's orchestration closely before breaking off in his own more radical direction.

And practically speaking, the three pieces cover the whole orchestra: Don Juan demands speed and precision, especially from the horns; the Dvořák leans on the woodwinds for its eerie theatrical effects; the Brahms needs nothing but pure, unified string tone and control. A genuinely well-chosen way for a new conductor to introduce himself to an orchestra — and to a hall that's heard a great many debuts before this one.

The vaporetto back from Rialto afterward came with the first real flicker of feeling like a local rather than a visitor — a nice note to end on, walking back past the winged lion at Campo Manin in the blue-hour dark. And, fittingly for a football-inflected trip, they made it back in time to catch Spain's second goal against Belgium in the World Cup quarter-final. A fine day around the islands, all told.

Saturday · 11 July

Venice to the Riviera del Brenta

← 10 Jul Top 12 Jul →

Live journaling today, more or less as it happens. Left Venice by ATVO bus and picked up the rental car at Marco Polo — the booked Mercedes-class wasn't available, so the consolation prize was a BMW X1 instead. Black, at least, which around here counts as a small mercy: nobody's roasting alive in a black car in July, they just look moderately more respectable doing it.

First stop was the town of Mira on the Riviera del Brenta, for lunch at the local cicchetteria, A Na' Certa — bread, cured meats, a sliced local cheese, a bit of tartare, and a fritter or two, the small-plates format doing exactly what it's meant to. It also produced the trip's first bottle of wine: a local Valpolicella Superiore for €14, which felt like a genuine bargain given that a Superiore back home at the Finnish Alko runs well north of €20.

A second pit stop followed at the Ipercity in Albignasego — less romantic than a Palladian villa, but necessary: socks and underwear restocked, and an extra piece of luggage picked up to get the growing wine collection and everything else home in one piece.

From Mira it was on through the winding hill roads around Valsanzibio — vineyards on both sides most of the way — to Le Volpi, tucked into the Euganean Hills. Fitting, given the name: "le volpi" means "the foxes," and sure enough there's a pair of them keeping watch over the place.

After settling in, the day wasn't quite done — a drive back out to Padova for pizza. Worth it for more than the food: a golden-hour shot of weathered statues against a glowing sky (the kind of light that makes even a stray TV antenna look intentional), and a stencil piece of street art — a boy watering a row of hearts instead of flowers — tucked into a wall along the way.

Pizza itself delivered — two full-sized pies, no regrets about portion sizes. Then back to Le Volpi in time to catch Norway play England, closing out a day that started with a wrong-class rental car and ended with vineyards, foxes, and football.

Sunday · 12 July

Down from the hills to Padova

← 11 Jul Top 13 Jul →

Waking up to a new day in a winery here in the hills is something else. The breakfast at Le Volpi was lovely, and the view nothing less than stunning.

Sunday's plan changed course: Padova instead of Arquà Petrarca. The rental car handled the hills better than expected, and the drive down wasn't nearly as bad as feared — plus the city had its own claim on the day regardless, with the university that hosted Mira for a semester meriting a proper, thorough visit.

The drive down came with its own experience: a steady scatter of bicyclists along the winding road through Fontanafredda, Galzignano Terme, and Torreglia — proof, once again, that everyone behind a wheel in these hills fancies themselves a Formula 1 driver working out an overtake.

The university itself did not disappoint. Palazzo del Bo — Padova's ancient seat of learning, founded in 1222 and one of the oldest universities in the world — greeted us through a stone archway with the EU, Italian, and Veneto flags overhead and Universitas degli Studi carved above the door. Galileo taught here for the better part of two decades; walking through the same gate felt like a different register of "old" than even Venice manages.

Inside, a frescoed staircase climbs toward the Rector's office — the sign at the top reads simply AL RETTORE — walls covered floor to ceiling in painted scholars, saints, and allegorical figures, the whole stairwell doubling as a history lesson on the way up.

Coffee afterward at Caffè Pedrocchi, the grand neoclassical café a short walk from the university — historically nicknamed "the café without doors." For decades after it opened in 1831 it genuinely had none, staying open around the clock for students, revolutionaries, and anyone else who wandered in off the street.

In Piazza dei Signori, the Torre dell'Orologio's astronomical clock turned into its own small puzzle — one of the oldest astronomical clocks in the world, dating to 1344, its blue-and-gold dial running the zodiac around a starry field. Except count the signs and one is conspicuously missing: Libra, dropped from the wheel — a holdover from antiquity, when the scales were folded into Scorpio's claws rather than treated as a sign of their own.

The scales turned up anyway, a few steps away on the flank of the Palazzo della Ragione — the enormous medieval hall known locally as Il Salone, its barrel-vaulted roof one of the largest unsupported roofs in Europe. A sundial mounted on the brickwork carries its own set of zodiac symbols, Libra included this time, closing the loop the clock tower left open.

The day closed at the Basilica di Sant'Antonio, known throughout Padova simply as Il Santo — a genuinely massive pile of brick and lead-grey domes, a silhouette owing more to Byzantium than to anything else on this trip so far, holding the tomb of St. Anthony of Padua at its center.

Prato della Valle itself, in the end, got only a supporting role: lunch stayed simple — a glass of fresh orange juice back at Pedrocchi, then pizza and salami picked up from the Despar next to the Prato della Valle parking — before pointing the car back up into the hills to Le Volpi.

Monte Fasolo sits close enough to Le Volpi that walking up seemed like a reasonable idea. It wasn't. The scorching heat and the rough dirt road would have called for proper hiking gear rather than our sandals and light sneakers, so we gave up on the sports part and hopped into the now-trusty diesel-powered Beamer instead.

It turned out we were the only ones at the winery — so we got a private tour of the cellars, the production facilities, and the tasting. The estate covers over 270 hectares, of which 110 are under vine and the rest given over to olive groves and other agricultural use. They grow several different grape varieties across many lines of wines, and the scale shows: tens of thousands of bottles a year, a seriously big producer for the area. The facilities are strikingly modern — the latest technology monitoring the wines resting in concrete storage, and oak barrels of equal quality alongside. The Rossi Luciani family owns both Monte Fasolo and Le Volpi, and has brought them back to life from neglect.

The tasting ran spumante, bianco, and two rossi, all from the Linea di Gi: the Fior d'Ape Metodo Classico — Vino Spumante di Qualità, Moscato Giallo 100%, Millesimato Extra Brut; the Àrio Colli Euganei DOC, Manzoni Bianco 100%; the 12 Mesi Colli Euganei DOC, Merlot 100%; and the 24 Mesi Colli Euganei DOC Rosso — Merlot 60%, Cabernet Sauvignon 30%, Cabernet Franc 10%.

The sparkling is nothing like sweet proseccos — made metodo classico, it's crisp, dry, and expressive. The white was floral and light, a spring breeze almost. Moving to the reds, the profile shifted to darker flowers, with passion and intensity, but still floral underneath. The reds are very modern too, and incredibly versatile — but from Monte Fasolo it's the sparklings that are the most interesting bottles.

After the tasting we took a stroll around the garden and had a look at the vines on top of the hill — they grow so tall here in Italy. The place carries a long history too: the old Oratory of San Gaetano da Thiene still stands, a reminder of the time when there was a monastery on the site.

By the time we came back down from the vineyards, the restaurant had opened — families, couples, and groups of friends filling the patio for dinner. We stayed, and put the sommelier's suggestions to work: the Fior d'Ape metodo classico with the antipasti (uovo poché for Mira, a tartare for me), then bigoli al ragù di corte with the 8 Mesi Cabernet for Mira, and costine di maiale — glazed with mustard and honey — with the Carmenère for me. The pairings were good: the Carmenère really fought off the pork fat, and the lighter 8 Mesi brought the light meat to life. We returned to Le Volpi with olive oil, spumante, and a bottle of the 24 Mesi. Overall, an amazing day.

Monday · 13 July

Monselice market and the Vignalta terroir

← 12 Jul Top 14 Jul →

Woke up to a fresh new morning and this amazing view — the citrus tree, a barrel by the railing, and the whole plain unrolling far below the terrace.

We decided on a quick drive down to check the market at Monselice. Another hot day, and our feet are aching after so many days of walking — first Venice, then yesterday Padova. A gelato in town, then back to Le Volpi for a cold plate for lunch.

Then off to Vignalta. The day was hot again, as we've now grown accustomed to in Veneto, and there was no discussion about whether we walk or drive. For some reason there seem to be no other people having tastings in the Colli Euganei just now, so we were treated to another private tour and tasting. A serious and quiet man called Michele took us in and started the tour with a quick look at the fields, to get an understanding of the terroir the vines grow in — then swiftly back inside, as the scorching afternoon sun was intense.

Vignalta's facilities are smaller than Monte Fasolo's, but they still produce a decent yield. Interestingly, they have left a wall of bare bedrock open in their new barrel storage, to visualize where the vines are extracting their flavors. Michele said the deepest roots reach ten meters down into the trachyte and limestone layers — incredible how strong the roots are. He also told us that the stone paving Piazza San Marco in Venice is Euganean trachyte.

The tasting normally runs sparkling, white, red, and a dessert wine — but now that we were alone, Michele let us pick the ones we wanted, so we switched the dessert for the Carmenère. What followed turned out to be a real introduction class to Vignalta's wines.

The sparkling was the Blanc de Noirs Brut Nature — Raboso Friularo — a wild and free sparkling, bubbles of wild northern acidic apples; a wine I'd pair with anchovy cicchetti. It was followed by the Gemola Chardonnay, a fun, creamy, easy-to-drink white. I made a fun little observation that this one was somehow American in profile — and Michele didn't just confirm it, he answered by producing the actual bottle from the shelf: a Mount Eden Vineyards Santa Cruz Mountains Chardonnay 2020. Lucio lives part-time in California, he explained, and it was there that Mount Eden inspired him to make something similar at Vignalta.

We moved on to the Carmenère, aged 24 months in French oak tonneaux, and this one was a party in the mouth — spicy and joyful, but with the elegance that seems to be the base note in every bottle here. Michele then pulled an everyday wine, the Rosso Riserva 2020 (Merlot–Cabernet): excellent, deep and serious but easy to drink. Followed by the signature bottle, the Gemola 2019 — Merlot 70%, Cabernet Franc 30%. The seriousness took a few steps up; now we're talking Christmas-table wine.

Somewhere in the discussions on grape varieties and taste profiles — and how much these reds resemble Bordeaux — Michele let us have an extra glass of the Cabernet Riserva 2011, 100% Cabernet Sauvignon, made simply because it was possible. This is not a wine for making money; this is a wine to keep and let develop.

One fascinating detail emerged along the way: the quiet, serious Michele guiding us turned out to be Michele Montecchio — the head winemaker of the whole show. No wonder the introduction class was this good; we'd had the professor himself.

We wandered off from the tasting with a bottle of the Carmenère and the Cab 2011 — the ones I described as the one to go to a game with, and the one to go home to for deep discussions.

The day finished with a dip in Le Volpi's infinity pool and a quick run to Arquà Petrarca, to Osteria al Guerriero. Lasagne paired with an Alla Costiera Biancone, and my anchovy pasta with the Alberto metodo classico. All in all, a good day.

Tuesday · 14 July

The last day: records, one more Padova, and home

← 13 Jul Top

The last day in Italy — there is no way to write that sentence that doesn't sting a little. One more breakfast on the Le Volpi terrace, then the final packing, which had become a ritual of its own: every bottle swaddled in shirts and cinched tight, a suitcase built around wine with some clothes along for the ride.

With the flight not leaving until 19:15, there was time for one last visit to Padova. Checkout, and down from the hills a final time — the winding road all ours now, the weekend's bicyclists gone back to their weekday lives.

In Padova we ducked into a record store and came out heavier: a compilation of Italian classics — La Canzone Italiana: In Giro per l'Italia — a Peppino di Capri album (…è mezzanotte), and the soundtrack of The Godfather Part II. After a light pizza lunch we went looking for an older chapter of this story: Mira's student dwelling near the Chiesa di San Pio X on Via Paolo Fambri, still standing exactly where she left it.

There was still plenty of time, so we took the slow way to the aeroporto, stopping for a slushy in Noale. The town kept its cards close — the Rocca was closed, so we admired it over the moat — but the piazza offered the trusty Beamer one last photogenic posting beneath the tower gate.

The final refuel came to €52 of diesel — a more than fair tab for a week of hauling us, our wine, and our ambitions up and down the Euganean Hills. Drop-off was painless, the same spot we'd collected it from, and the attendant was friendly in that unhurried Italian way: before the keys had even changed hands he was telling us about his nephew's winery in the Colli Euganei, for when we come back. When. Not if.

We had budgeted generously for the drop-off and spent the surplus waiting for the bag drop to open. From there, the business upgrade earned its keep twice over: a hefty security queue skipped in a stride, and a delayed flight waited out in the lounge, the lagoon shimmering on the far side of the glass.

The flight itself passed smoothly, the service up front feeling more like a last course than a commute — a worthy end to the trip. We landed into a Helsinki dusk that never quite committed to darkness, two suitcases heavier and already talking about when.

Buon viaggio — Venice to the vineyards.
